Fourier’s Law of Heat Conduction

A Comprehensive Study of One-Dimensional Heat Flow

1. Fundamental Principles

Fourier’s Law is an empirical principle that describes the rate of heat transfer. It posits that heat flux is proportional to the negative gradient of temperature.

  • Directionality: Heat naturally flows from higher to lower temperature regions.
  • Linearity: The flow rate is linear relative to the temperature gradient steepness.
  • Thermal Conductivity (κ): An intrinsic material property defining energy transfer efficiency.

2. Mathematical Formulation

For a one-dimensional system, the quantity of heat flow q from area A is expressed as:

q = -k · A · dT/dx
The Negative Sign: Indicates that heat flows in the direction of decreasing temperature (as distance increases, temperature decreases).

3. Application: Radial Heat Flow

In a cylindrical pipe, the surface area A changes with the radius x.

q = [2πk(T0 - T1)] / ln(x1/x0)

This integrated solution represents positive flow from hot (T0) to cold (T1).

4. Key Insights

  • Steady State: Assumes temperature at any point remains constant over time.
  • Microscopic Origin: Heat is carried by phonons and free electrons.
  • Limits: Law may fail at the nanoscale or under high-frequency pulses.
